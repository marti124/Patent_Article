[LED patents]
White LED architecture (blue GaN/InGaN chip + yellow YAG phosphor) developed at Nichia Corporation; efficient blue LED by Shuji Nakamura, early 1990s; first commercial white LED, 1996. Nakamura shared the 2014 Nobel Prize in Physics. Nichia held the patents (profits reported ~¥120.8 billion / ~$120 million over their life; Nakamura initially awarded ~$200) and litigated to defend them, including suit against Cree Inc.: Science (2001), "Japanese Inventor Sues Company"; EE Times (2001); Wikipedia, "Shuji Nakamura"; U.S. patent record (blue-LED/phosphor lineage). U.S. utility patents run 20 years from filing; core 1990s LED patents have largely expired in recent years.
View source →
[laser lighting]
Laser-diode white light (blue laser diode + phosphor). Shuji Nakamura's post-LED work and SoraaLaser / SLD Laser; Nakamura's public statements that laser diodes are the future of lighting. Nichia GigaWhite white laser module (blue laser + phosphor), developed for automotive headlights, specialty and industrial lighting; Nichia 2016 statement prioritizing laser diodes and citing a "comprehensive white light laser patent portfolio." BMW laser headlights (first production deployment). Sources: Nichia product literature (GigaWhite); SLD Laser / Nakamura interviews; trade coverage of BMW laser headlights, 2014–2016. Current deployments are automotive/specialty, not general home illumination.
View source →
[lighting standards]
U.S. lighting efficiency timeline: Energy Independence and Security Act of 2007 (EISA), general-service lamp standards; traditional incandescent phase-out 2012–2014 (100W in 2012 to 40W by 2014). 45 lm/W "backstop" standard: DOE action 2017; rolled back under the Trump administration; reinstated April 2022 (Biden DOE); retailer enforcement from Aug. 1, 2023 — effectively ending incandescent (~15 lm/W) and halogen (~25 lm/W). Proposed >120 lm/W standard (Dec. 2022) would end CFLs (~60 lm/W), ~2028. Sources: U.S. Department of Energy rulemakings and press releases; EISA 2007 (Pub. L. 110-140); contemporaneous trade and news coverage (2022–2023).
